Adjoint Representations

Published on June 18, 2026 · 5 min read

Spectral sequences are notoriously intimidating when you first encounter them in algebraic topology or homological algebra. They are often described as a "black box" machinery of pages computing successive approximations of homology groups. Today, I want to share a geometric scratchpad model I use to keep track of the differentials without losing my sanity.

Recall that a homological spectral sequence consists of a collection of abelian groups (or modules) $E_{p,q}^r$ for $r \geq 1$, equipped with differentials of bidegree $(-r, r-1)$:

$$d^r: E_{p,q}^r \to E_{p-r, \, q+r-1}^r$$

The key property is that each page is the homology of the preceding one with respect to this differential. That is, we have the core relationship:

Definition: The next page of the sequence is determined by the kernel and image of the current page's differential: $$E_{p,q}^{r+1} \cong \frac{\ker(d^r: E_{p,q}^r \to E_{p-r, q+r-1}^r)}{\text{im}(d^r: E_{p+r, q-r+1}^r \to E_{p,q}^r)}$$

The Grid Mapping

To visualize the movement of the $d^r$ maps, think of pages as a stack of grids moving through time $r$. On the $E^1$ page, the differentials are purely vertical, dropping down by 1 unit ($d^1: E_{p,q}^1 \to E_{p-1, q}^1$).

By the time we hit the $E^2$ page, the arrows pivot. They shift left by 2 units and up by 1 unit. As $r \to \infty$, the arrows flatten out completely, eventually leaving us with the stable $E^\infty$ page which allows us to read off the filtration of our target object.

In the next post, I will look at how this machinery plays out explicitly for the Serre spectral sequence of a fibration $F \to E \to B$.
